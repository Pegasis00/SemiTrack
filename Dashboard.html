Executive command center

India's semiconductor import bill is scaling faster than any visible substitution signal.

The 1995-2024 trade record points to a sharper post-2018 import regime, overwhelming HS 8542 dominance, and deeper dependence on a narrow East Asian supplier base. This platform is structured as a decision surface: macro trend first, supply-chain structure second, risk concentration third, and year-wise diagnostics for deeper policy reading.

Macro Trends

Import scale has shifted into a much steeper post-2018 regime.

Historical real and nominal imports are shown alongside the ARIMAX baseline for 2025-2027 so the current ramp can be read against the longer arc.

1995-2027
India semiconductor import trajectory
Real 2015 USD, nominal USD, and ARIMAX baseline forecast with confidence band.
What this means

Imports trend upward across the full sample, with visible setbacks around 2008 during the global financial crisis and 2020 during COVID disruption. The slope steepens after 2018, consistent with India's electronics boom and deeper dependence on imported semiconductor value. Policy implication: domestic capacity still trails downstream demand growth.

Post-2017 acceleration index
Real and nominal imports indexed to 2017=100 to isolate the structural break.
What this means

Using 2017 as a common base makes the regime break easier to see: both real and nominal imports re-rate sharply after 2018. The 2020 dip is temporary, while 2021-22 keeps the series on a higher path. Policy implication: planning assumptions should start from the new regime, not the old base.

What this means

China, Taiwan, and South Korea dominate the 2024 sourcing stack. This reflects where fabrication and packaging depth sit globally, not genuine diversification for India. Policy implication: risk reduction must start with dual-sourcing and trusted-partner depth, not just more import volume.

Growth Dynamics

Demand remains volatile even while the underlying import base keeps scaling.

The first chart shows year-on-year real growth, while the second smooths the series into rolling volatility so crisis periods and post-2018 instability are easier to isolate.

Volatility and acceleration
Year-on-year real growth
Growth in real 2015 USD terms; negative bars mark contraction years.
What this means

Growth turns sharply negative around 2008, 2013-15, and 2020, then rebounds hard in 2021-22 during the chip-shortage recovery window. The volatility does not cancel the long-run climb; it rides on top of it. Policy implication: resilience planning has to manage both shocks and structurally rising import demand.

Growth rate volatility
Three-year rolling volatility of real growth, highlighting shock windows.
What this means

Rolling volatility spikes around the 2008 crisis, the 2018 structural break, and the 2020-22 COVID plus chip-shortage period. Volatility cools by 2024, but from a far larger import base than before. Policy implication: India needs a resilience strategy that assumes continued growth, not a return to the older low-base regime.
